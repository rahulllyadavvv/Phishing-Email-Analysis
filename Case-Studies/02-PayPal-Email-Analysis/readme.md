
# Phishing Email Report — "PayPal $90 Reward" Email


**What this email is trying to do:** It poses as a PayPal reward notification offering an exclusive "$90 Promo Reward" for filling out a marketing survey, pushing the reader to click through to claim it.

**Final Answer: This is a Phishing Email**

## 1. Email Header Check

| What we checked | What we found | What it means |
|---|---|---|
| SPF | none | The website lsvaim.net has no SPF record at all. So we can't trust that this email really came from them. |
| DKIM | none (not signed) | This email has no DKIM signature at all. |
| DMARC | none, and compauth=fail | This domain has no DMARC rulebook (none) and compauth came back fail. |

**Important Point to Note:** This email uses three different domains that don't match each other:
1. From header domain: poumzenuhs.com
2. Return-Path / mailfrom domain: lsvaim.net
3. HELO (sending) domain: munertiaseer.co.uk

A real PayPal email should use PayPal's own domain everywhere. Having three unrelated domains is a big red flag.

## 2. Sender / Domain Check

The From field says "Paypal," but the real email address is 0a7pz@poumzenuhs.com a strange, random domain, nowhere close to PayPal at all. I looked up poumzenuhs.com on VirusTotal, and it carries a special tag: **DGA** meaning a domain name generated by software, not manually chosen by a human. Attackers use DGA domains so it's harder for security tools to block them all at once. This is stronger evidence than a simple "0 detections" result would suggest.

The Return-Path domain, lsvaim.net, is also a different, unrelated domain, with 0/91 detections on VirusTotal but no SPF record of its own.

## 3. Email Body Check

The email tries to trick the reader with: "You have been selected to get an exclusive $90 offer!" a classic social engineering trick that makes people click without thinking. Real companies rarely email random free-money offers out of nowhere.

## 4. Links and Attachment Analysis

There are three links in the email body, and all were flagged as dangerous:

- hxxps://t[.]co/iRkfI59fEa a URL shortener (t.co, owned by Twitter/X), used three separate times in the email. Tracing where it redirects shows it goes to winkflames[.]com, a scam survey landing page, not PayPal.
- hxxps://t[.]co/fSOqXAjaz0 the same shortener trick, also flagged by VirusTotal.
- hxxp://dtherhproblem[.]us/su46864CCVDf27537896ibph1371191AkO68049nZpa3271 VirusTotal shows Fortinet flagged this URL directly as Phishing, and alphaMountain.ai flagged it as Suspicious. The domain was registered nearly 2 years before this campaign, but the specific URL path was submitted only 13 days before analysis — an aged-domain-reuse tactic that lets a new phishing link inherit an older domain's less-suspicious reputation.

## 5. Hidden Artifacts

A tracking pixel — hxxp://vertuoldmuneted[.]com/track/o46864kOPuY27537896eUYw1371191AuU68049WFee3271 — embeds the numbers **27537896** and **1371191**.

**Final Verdict:** This email pretends to be from PayPal offering free reward money, but everything about it is fake SPF, DKIM, and DMARC all failed or were missing, the sender domain (poumzenuhs.com) is flagged as DGA, and all three links lead to phishing or scam pages confirmed by VirusTotal. The tracking pixel also embeds a numeric ID that reappears in a different case later in this series. So the conclusion is it's a Phishing email.
